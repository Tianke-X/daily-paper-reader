Abstract 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) typi-cally assumes that external knowledge is free, but many high-quality sources are paywalled, licensed, restricted, or otherwise costly to ac-cess. We introduce cost-aware RAG, a setting where retrieved evidence is assigned access-cost tiers and systems must answer under an explicit evidence-access budget. We instanti-ate this setting by augmenting MS MARCO v2.1 with access-friction tiers and evaluate budgeted evidence selection across general-domain and domain-specific QA benchmarks. Our results show that static selection is brit-tle: no fixed selector uniformly dominates, and larger budgets do not reliably improve answer quality, even when costly evidence is domain-matched. We then study agen-tic cost-aware RAG, where an LLM decides when to retrieve, which tier to access, and when to stop. Agents show strong promise as adaptive evidence-acquisition controllers, but their behavior remains highly model- and task-dependent. These findings suggest that cost-aware evidence acquisition is a central challenge for the next generation of RAG sys-tems. All code and data are available at https: //github.com/Mignonmy/Cost-Aware .

1 Introduction 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems are usually evaluated as if external knowledge were freely and uniformly accessible. In practice, how-ever, many high-quality sources are restricted, li-censed, paywalled, or otherwise costly to access, including academic journals, clinical references, professional databases, licensed code repositories, and proprietary business resources. This makes retrieval not only a relevance-ranking problem, but also a resource-allocation problem: a system must decide which evidence is worth acquiring under a

This access cost is distinct from commonly studied RAG constraints such as computation time, context length, or token budgets (Jeong et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025). Those constraints limit how much evidence can be processed once it is available; access costs deter-mine which evidence can be obtained in the first place. Cost-aware RAG therefore requires estimat-ing the value of evidence relative to its cost, rather than optimizing retrieval relevance or generation quality alone. These tiers serve as coarse proxies for access fric-tion, distinguishing freely accessible sources, gen-eral open-web sources, and higher-friction profes-sional or restricted sources. As shown in Figure 1, each evidence item has an access cost, and the system must select evidence under an explicit bud-get before passing it to the generator. We evalu-ate this framework on general-domain QA bench-marks, including HotpotQA, Natural Questions (NQ), and TriviaQA, and domain-specific medi-cal QA benchmarks, including MedQA-US and MMLU-Med. For the medical setting, we augment the high-cost tier with domain-matched textbook evidence, allowing us to test whether costly spe-cialized sources help when relevant knowledge is concentrated in higher-friction resources. We com-pare static evidence-selection strategies, including cost-agnostic top-k, relevance-only selection, cost-aware greedy selection, knapsack-based optimiza-tion, and redundancy-aware methods. Our results show that cost-aware RAG does not admit a single fixed selection rule. No static selec-tor uniformly dominates across datasets, budgets, and backbone models, and larger access budgets do not reliably improve answer quality. Since the gen-erator receives a bounded evidence context, increas-ing the budget mainly changes the composition of the selected evidence, allowing low-cost passages to be replaced by higher-cost ones. Even in the medical setting, where costly evidence is domain-matched, a more expensive evidence mix is not nec-essarily more useful. These findings motivate an adaptive formulation. Rather than assigning every query the same budget and applying a fixed selec-tor, a cost-aware system should decide during in-ference whether more evidence is needed, whether higher-cost evidence is worth acquiring, and when the current context is sufficient. We therefore study an agentic formulation of cost-aware RAG, where an LLM acts as an evidence-acquisition controller. Agents can substantially improve the performance– cost tradeoff with some backbones, but their behav-ior remains model- and task-dependent, showing that current LLMs are still imperfect cost-sensitive evidence controllers.

2 Constructing a Cost-Aware Retrieval Corpus 

2.1 Cost-annotated corpus. 

We build on the MS MARCO v2.1 document col-lection (Nguyen et al., 2016), which consists of web-sourced passages with associated queries and source URLs. We keep the original queries un-changed, and augment each evidence item with an access-cost label inferred from its originating do-main. These costs are intended to model relative access friction rather than literal monetary prices. Specifically, each source domain is assigned to a discrete cost tier, summarized in Table 1, rang-ing from open community resources to general open-web content and higher-friction curated, pro-fessional, or restricted sources. This construction enables budget-constrained retrieval and evalua-tion while preserving the original MS MARCO evidence and supervision. 

2.2 Domain distribution analysis. 

We first analyze the source-domain distribu-tion of the MS MARCO v2.1 segment corpus. The corpus contains 113,520,750 segments from 1,035,806 unique domains, and the distribution is highly skewed. The most frequent domain, 

en.wikipedia.org , contributes 7,006,110 seg-ments, corresponding to 6.17% of the corpus, while the top 30 domains together account for 19,178,928 segments, or 16.89% of all segments. This skew motivates a domain-level annotation strategy: a relatively small number of high-coverage domains accounts for a substantial fraction of the corpus, while most domains occur only in the long tail. 

2.3 LLM-based domain classification. 

Since manually labeling more than one million domains is impractical, we annotate the highest-coverage domains using an LLM-as-judge proce-dure. We select the top 20,000 domains by seg-ment count, which together cover 82,604,646 seg-ments, or 72.77% of the full corpus. Given the tier definitions in Table 1, GPT-4.1-mini assigns each domain to an access-friction tier based on the domain identity and its likely accessibility charac-teristics. To avoid forcing arbitrary assignments, the model is also allowed to abstain when the do-main cannot be confidently classified; abstentions are rare in practice (around 0.3%). The initial anno-tation used a four-way rubric separating curated or professional sources from high-friction restricted sources. Under this rubric, the top 20,000 domains were classified as follows: 1,904 domains in Tier 0 (9.52%), 14,350 in Tier 1 (71.75%), 2,715 in Tier 2 (13.58%), and 1,031 in Tier 3 (5.16%). Be-cause the two highest-friction classes are relatively sparse, we merge the original Tiers 2 and 3 into the final Tier 2 used in our experiments. This yields a three-tier structure that preserves the distinction between free, low-cost, and costly evidence while making the highest-cost tier sufficiently populated for evaluation. 

2.4 Long-tail imputation and final tier distribution. 

The remaining domains form a large long tail with low individual coverage. Rather than querying the LLM for every rare domain, we impute their tiers by sampling from the empirical tier proportions observed among the annotated top 20,000 domains. After propagating domain labels to all segments and merging the two highest-friction tiers, the fi-nal MS MARCO-only corpus contains 36,547,305 Tier 0 segments (32.19%), 62,632,643 Tier 1 seg-ments (55.17%), and 14,340,802 Tier 2 segments (12.64%), as shown in Table 2. 3 Experimental Setting 

3.1 Models 

We evaluate two open-weight language models: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Touvron et al., 2023) and Qwen3-8B (Team, 2025). This allows us to compare cost-aware retrieval behavior across two widely used model families at a similar param-eter scale. During retrieval, Qwen3-embedding-0.6B (Zhang et al., 2025) is used to retrieve query-related documents, and the number of retrieval doc-uments is limited to the top 30. 

3.2 Datasets Retrieval corpus We use the cost-annotated MS MARCO v2.1 corpus as our main retrieval source (Nguyen et al., 2016). Each passage in-herits an access-cost label from its source domain, capturing relative access friction rather than literal monetary price. In our experiments, the final cost tiers are mapped to costs 0, 1, and 4, corresponding to free, low-cost, and costly evidence, respectively. 

Evaluation benchmarks We evaluate on five question-answering benchmarks that span both open-domain and domain-specific settings. For open-domain QA, we use HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), Natural Questions (NQ) (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), and TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017). These benchmarks require broad web knowledge and cover different retrieval demands, from relatively direct factoid questions to multi-hop reasoning over multiple pieces of evidence. For domain-specific QA, we use MedQA-US (Jin et al., 2020) and MMLU-Med (Xiong et al., 2024). To create a more realistic high-friction retrieval scenario for the medical benchmarks, we aug-ment the Tier-2 evidence pool for MedQA-US and MMLU-Med with Textbooks (Jin et al., 2020), the large medical textbook corpus released with MedQA (Jin et al., 2020). We treat this corpus as costly domain-specific evidence, simulating expert medical sources such as scientific articles, text-books, and clinical reference materials that are more expensive to access but may provide higher-quality information. This setup allows us to test cost-aware evidence selection both when relevant knowledge is broadly available on the web and when it is concentrated in specialized, high-friction sources. We provide further experimental details in Appendix B. 

3.3 Budgeted Retrieval Protocol 

For each query q, we retrieve a candidate set of passages and select a subset to provide to the gen-erator under an evidence-access budget B. Each candidate passage di is associated with a relevance score vi, estimated by the similarity score between the passage and the query, and an access cost ci,determined by its source cost tier. Unless oth-erwise stated, the generator receives at most 30 pieces of evidence per question. When the budget is exhausted before reaching this evidence limit, the remaining context slots can be filled with the highest-ranked Tier-0 evidence, since these pas-sages incur no additional access cost. We evaluate answer quality as a function of the available budget, measuring how different selection strategies trade off relevance, cost, and redundancy.

4 Results 

We first ask whether cost-aware evidence selection can be solved by a fixed budgeted selector. The results suggest not: across datasets, budgets, and backbone models, no single rule consistently dom-inates, and increasing the evidence-access budget often fails to improve performance. These find-ings motivate a more adaptive formulation, where the system decides how much to spend and which evidence tier to access on a per-query basis. 

No fixed selector uniformly dominates. Fig-ure 2 shows that the best selector depends strongly on the dataset, budget, and backbone model. More budget is not necessarily better. Since the generator receives a fixed evidence window, in-creasing B mainly changes the composition of the selected evidence rather than the amount of context. The relevant question is therefore whether a more expensive evidence mix improves answer quality. It does not reliably do so. Across the five selec-tors and five datasets, none of the 25 Qwen3-8B curves is monotonically non-decreasing over bud-gets {0, 5, 10 , 15 , 20 , 25 , 30 }, and the same holds for Llama3.1-8B-Instruct. Moreover, only 6 out of 25 Qwen3-8B curves and 4 out of 25 Llama3.1-8B-Instruct curves achieve their best finite-budget score at B = 30 . This remains true even in the domain-specific benchmarks, where the high-cost tier includes domain-matched medical evi-dence. With Qwen3-8B on MMLU-Med, Rele-vance reaches 84.00 F1 at B = 5 but drops to 79.00 at B = 30 , while Greedy reaches 84.00 at 

B = 10 and B = 25 but drops to 78.00 at B = 30 .On MedQA, Relevance and Knapsack also peak before the largest budget. Thus, even when costly evidence is plausibly useful, simply allowing more of it into the context is not enough. 

Budget changes evidence composition differ-ently across domains. Figure 3 helps explain the non-monotonic trends. In the open-domain setting, represented by NQ, larger budgets do not necessarily lead to substantially greater use of the highest-cost tier; many methods continue to rely on lower-cost evidence. This suggests that general-knowledge questions can often be answered from open or low-cost sources. In contrast, for MedQA, where the costly tier contains domain-specialized medical evidence, higher budgets often lead selec-tors to use more high-cost evidence. The value of expensive evidence is therefore task-dependent: conservative policies may miss useful specialized evidence, while aggressive policies may waste bud-get when low-cost evidence is sufficient. 

Reducing the number of evidence passages is not sufficient. As shown in Table 12 and 13, the cost-agnostic top-k baseline controls for context size by providing only the k highest-ranked passages, without considering access cost. If the weakness of fixed selectors were simply that they include too many passages, then some fixed k should perform consistently well. The results do not support this explanation. Top-k sometimes benefits from using fewer than 30 passages, but no value of k is con-sistently strong across datasets or models. With Qwen3-8B, the best top-k scores on HotpotQA and NQ are 37.67 and 40.09 F1, below the best budgeted-selector scores of 40.90 and 45.19 F1. With Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, top-k also falls short on HotpotQA and NQ. Thus, shorter contexts alone do not explain the gains; the harder problem is de-ciding which evidence is worth including and from which cost tier. 

Implication: cost-aware RAG requires adaptive allocation. Taken together, these results show that cost-aware evidence selection is difficult to reduce to a single fixed rule. The best selector varies across datasets, models, and budgets; larger budgets do not reliably help; and the usefulness of costly evidence depends on the domain and query. This motivates a sequential formulation in which the system assesses the evidence already acquired, decides whether it is sufficient, and determines whether additional or higher-cost evidence is worth the expense. We therefore turn to an agentic formu-lation of cost-aware RAG, where evidence acqui-sition is treated as an online decision process: the model must decide not only what to retrieve, but also when to spend, where to spend, and when to stop.

At inference time, the agent must decide whether to retrieve, which cost tier to access, and when the accumulated evidence is sufficient to answer. This setting is more flexible than static selection, but it also introduces new failure modes: the agent may stop prematurely, spend too aggressively, or retrieve from an inappropriate tier. We therefore view agentic RAG not as a complete solution, but as a probe of whether current LLMs can act as cost-sensitive evidence controllers.

Per-query agents can be efficient evidence con-trollers. Figure 5 compares per-query agents with fixed budgeted selectors. The main result is that agentic acquisition can often match or exceed fixed selectors while using far fewer passages and much lower realized cost. This improvement is not explained by shorter contexts alone: as shown in Section 4, cost-agnostic top-k selection does not consistently perform well. The clearest gains come from Qwen3-8B. On NQ, the agent reaches 49.93 F1 with only 0.50 average cost and 2.47 retrieved passages, outperforming all fixed selectors while spending far less than the high-budget settings. On TriviaQA, it reaches 66.73 F1 with 0.52 cost and 2.60 passages, and on MMLU-Med it reaches 85.00 F1 with 2.90 cost and only 1.14 passages. On MedQA, it obtains 68.00 F1 with 5.08 cost and 1.28 passages, competitive with the best fixed selec-tors despite using a much smaller evidence context. These results suggest that the agent is not simply retrieving more evidence; rather, it often stops after acquiring a small amount of useful evidence. 

Agentic cost awareness is model- and task-dependent. Agentic control is not automatically cost-aware. HotpotQA remains difficult: with Qwen3-8B and B = 20 , the agent reaches only 30.34 F1, below the best fixed selector. This sug-gests that multi-hop questions may require more than repeated retrieval with the original query, such as query decomposition, sub-question planning, or targeted query rewriting. Backbone choice also matters . Llama3.1-8B-Instruct can achieve com-petitive scores, but often by spending much more. With B = 20 , it reaches 46.77 F1 on NQ using 13.47 average cost and 12.54 passages, and 68.40 F1 on TriviaQA using 11.53 cost and 11.53 pas-sages. On MedQA, it reaches 60.00 F1 with 18.39 cost, and on MMLU-Med it reaches only 72.00 F1, substantially below the best fixed selectors. In con-trast to Qwen3-8B, the Llama agent often treats the available budget as a resource to consume rather than a constraint to manage. Thus, the key chal-lenge is not only choosing the right tier, but also estimating when the current evidence is sufficient. 

Budget sensitivity reveals evidence-sufficiency failures. A cost-aware agent should not spend more merely because more budget is available. We therefore vary the per-query budget on NQ and MedQA, covering both open-domain and domain-specific settings. Figure 6 shows that increasing the budget has limited effect on final F1, but substan-tially changes spending behaviour. Qwen3-8B ex-hibits relatively stable stopping behaviour. Increas-ing the budget from B = 20 to B = 30 changes its average cost from 0.50 to 1.06 on NQ and from 5.08 to 4.72 on MedQA, while F1 changes from 49.93 to 45.20 and from 68.00 to 69.00. In con-trast, Llama3.1-8B-Instruct spends much more as the budget increases, without corresponding gains: from B = 20 to B = 30 , its cost rises from 13.47 to 15.54 on NQ and from 18.39 to 27.01 on MedQA, while F1 drops from 46.77 to 42.89 and from 60.00 to 56.00. These trends indicate that evidence-sufficiency estimation is a central bottle-neck for agentic cost-aware RAG. Representative trajectories are provided in Appendix C and a cor-responding case study comparing this behaviour across both models is presented in Appendix D. 

Shared-budget allocation probes workload-level planning. We next evaluate shared-budget allo-cation as a diagnostic for workload-level planning. Unlike the per-query setting, the agent receives a batch of questions and must decide in advance which ones deserve more budget. For Qwen3-8B, this improves the performance–cost tradeoff on most datasets: compared with the per-query 

B = 20 agent, it reduces cost and passage usage on HotpotQA, TriviaQA, MedQA, and MMLU-Med while improving or maintaining F1. For example, TriviaQA improves from 66.73 to 70.27 F1 while cost drops from 0.52 to 0.33; MedQA improves from 68.00 to 71.00 F1 while cost drops from 5.08 to 4.38; and MMLU-Med maintains 85.00 F1 while reducing cost from 2.90 to 2.56. However, the benefit is not uniform: on NQ, Qwen spends more while performing worse, and for Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, lower cost and passage usage often come with lower F1. Thus, shared-budget alloca-tion can help when the model estimates question difficulty well, but it also introduces a new failure mode: the agent must predict before retrieval which questions are worth spending on. 

Takeaway. Agentic frameworks naturally fit cost-aware RAG: each retrieval decision can be con-ditioned on the question, the evidence collected so far, and the remaining budget. This adaptivity can improve the performance–cost tradeoff when the model recognizes that a small amount of ev-idence is sufficient. However, current agents re-main unreliable cost-sensitive controllers: their behaviour varies with the backbone, task struc-ture, and evidence-sufficiency judgments. Cost-aware RAG therefore highlights a capability un-derexplored in standard RAG: deciding whether additional evidence is worth its access cost. 

6 Related Work 

RAG under resource constraints. Most RAG work assumes that retrieved evidence is freely ac-cessible (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020; Izacard and Grave, 2021). Recent work stud-ies resource-constrained RAG by limiting context length, token usage, latency, inference-time com-putation, or the number of retrieved chunks (Jeong et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025). Re-lated budgeted-RAG work attaches cost to later stages of the pipeline: CORAG selects among already-retrieved chunks under a cost constraint (Wang et al., 2024), while Chunk-as-a-Service stud-ies pricing for chunks used to enrich prompts (Al-Maliki et al., 2026). Our setting moves the cost ear-lier: evidence may be costly to access depending on its source, so the system must decide whether a higher-cost source is worth acquiring before its evidence can be used. 

Adaptive and agentic RAG. Adaptive and agen-tic RAG methods make retrieval conditional rather than fixed: Adaptive-RAG routes queries by es-timated complexity (Jeong et al., 2024), Active-RAG/FLARE and Self-RAG trigger retrieval dur-ing generation (Jiang et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2024), and ReAct interleaves reasoning with actions and observations (Yao et al., 2023). These methods motivate our agentic formulation, but they do not study evidence access costs. In our setting, the model must decide not only whether more evidence is needed, but whether it is worth acquiring from a low- or high-cost source. 

7 Conclusion 

We introduced cost-aware RAG, where evidence carries source-dependent access costs and retrieval becomes a budgeted acquisition problem. Across open-domain and domain-specific QA, fixed se-lectors are brittle: no rule consistently dominates, and higher budgets do not reliably improve answer quality. Agentic RAG offers a promising alterna-tive by deciding when to retrieve, which tier to access, and when to stop, but current agents remain highly model- and task-dependent. 8 Limitations 

Simulated access costs. Our access-cost tiers are proxies for relative access friction rather than direct measurements of monetary cost, licensing restric-tions, latency, or institutional access. We assign costs at the domain level and map them to a small number of discrete tiers, which enables large-scale evaluation but cannot capture variation within the same source. 

Automatic tier annotation. Our cost annotations rely on automatic domain classification and long-tail imputation. The LLM judge may misclassify some domains, and imputed labels for rare domains may not reflect their true access properties. These errors could affect the apparent value of high-cost evidence and the behavior of cost-aware selectors. 

Evaluation scope. Our evaluation is limited to five QA benchmarks and sampled subsets. This al-lows controlled comparisons across selectors, bud-gets, and backbones, but leaves open how stable the conclusions are on larger samples, additional do-mains, and different answer formats. The medical benchmarks should be viewed as domain-specific QA testbeds rather than evidence of clinical readi-ness. 

Simplified retrieval environment. The agent re-trieves from a fixed candidate pool using the orig-inal question and receives one passage at a time from a selected tier. It does not perform query rewriting, sub-question decomposition, source ver-ification, or multi-query search, which likely limits performance on multi-hop questions. 

Zero-shot agent control. Our agentic controller is evaluated in a zero-shot prompting regime with two open-weight backbones. The results show that agentic cost-aware RAG can be efficient, but stop-ping and spending behavior remains highly model-dependent. We therefore view the agentic results as a diagnostic of current LLMs as cost-sensitive evi-dence controllers, rather than as a fully optimized policy.

D Case Studies: Agents’ Cost-aware Decision Making 

We illustrate how the agent processes tasks. In the first case, as shown in Table 6, the agent first iden-tifies that the question requires external evidence about a specific historical election and therefore begins with tier 0, the free retrieval source. When the first retrieved passage fails to provide informa-tion about the target year, it continues retrieving from tier 0, since the needed fact is likely available in encyclopedic sources. The second retrieval pro-vides sufficient evidence that Eliot Cutler finished ahead of Libby Mitchell in Maine’s 2010 guberna-torial election, allowing the agent to answer cor-rectly. This example shows that the agent performs tier-aware evidence acquisition. It keeps retrieving when evidence is insufficient, avoids unnecessarily expensive sources, and reaches the correct answer. In the second case, as shown in Table 7, the agent handles a question that requires specific and up-to-date factual evidence about Mount Ruapehu’s most recent eruption. The agent first retrieves from tier 0, since the question asks for a factual answer that may be available in free community resources. However, the initial passages only provide histori-cal information, rather than the exact date of the lat-est eruption. After multiple unsuccessful attempts from tier 0 and tier 1, the agent decides to explore a higher-cost tier, since cheaper sources have not yet provided the required evidence and the remain-ing budget is still sufficient. Although the tier 2 retrieval also fails to directly answer the question, the agent does not stop prematurely. Instead, it continues retrieval and eventually finds a useful tier 0 passage that explicitly states the last eruption date as 25 September 2007. This case shows that the agent can adaptively balance cost and evidence quality. It incurs a total cost of 7 and switches across all three tiers, but eventually retrieves the necessary passage and stops retrieving once suffi-cient evidence is found. In the third case, as shown in Table 8, the agent handles a domain-specific medical question and correctly identifies tier 2 as the appropriate source for professional medical knowledge. Since the re-trieved passages do not fully specify the protocol for this situation, the agent remains uncertain and continues searching until the budget is exhausted. With no remaining budget, the agent then combines the incomplete evidence with its own parametric knowledge and reasoning to make a correct final decision. This case shows that the agent can iden-tify the appropriate knowledge tier, understand the trade-off between task difficulty and budget, and continue evidence acquisition when confidence is insufficient. We further demonstrate how the agent performs flexible budget allocation under the shared-budget setting. As shown in Table 9, the agent first esti-mates the domain, difficulty, and likely retrieval tier of each question. It assigns smaller budgets to questions that can likely be answered by public or low-cost sources, while reserving larger budgets for questions that require more specific historical or scientific knowledge. The agent then iteratively adjusts the allocation until the total budget exactly matches the shared budget constraint. This case shows that the agent can reason about the relative difficulty of multiple questions and distribute the available budget accordingly. We further compare cost-aware behaviours across different backbone models. As shown in Ta-ble 10 and Table 11, both agents identify the ques-tion as requiring domain-specific medical knowl-edge and therefore select tier 2 for retrieval. How-ever, they differ substantially in when to stop re-trieving. The agent with Qwen3-8B retrieves only one tier 2 passage, recognizes that the evidence is sufficient, and stops with a total cost of 4. In contrast, the agent with Llama3.1-8B-Instruct re-peatedly retrieves from tier 2 despite already having relevant evidence, and only answers after exhaust-ing the full budget of 40. This comparison suggests that cost-aware behaviour is strongly influenced by the backbone model. 0
