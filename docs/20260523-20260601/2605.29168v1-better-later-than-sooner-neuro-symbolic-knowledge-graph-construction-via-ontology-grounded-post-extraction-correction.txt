Abstract 

Question answering (QA) is a core challenge in AI, particularly for complex queries requiring multi-hop reasoning across documents, or sym-bolic operations like aggregation or exhaustive listing. Retrieval-augmented generation has become the dominant approach to QA, with recent graph-based variants addressing part of these issues by organizing knowledge to bet-ter support compositional questions. However, most textual graph-based RAG methods still lack the structure needed for symbolic opera-tions useful to answer complex questions re-liably. This motivates symbolic graph-based approaches, which extract knowledge graphs (KGs) whose relations are logic predicates that enable SQL-like querying. Yet these pipelines typically use LLMs for KG extraction, which can introduce consistency issues, where ex-tracted facts may violate commonsense ontol-ogy constraints. We propose a neuro-symbolic framework for ontology-grounded KG con-struction combining open-domain extraction, embedding-based canonicalization of types and predicates, and targeted LLM-based correction of ontology violations. By deferring correc-tions to a post-extraction stage, our method avoids repeated LLM calls, substantially reduc-ing token usage while improving KG consis-tency and preserving downstream QA quality. Finally, we show that the extracted KGs are well suited for symbolic querying by measur-ing the occurrence of SPARQL graph patterns.

method for question answering (QA) is retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), which decomposes the task into two steps: retrieving text fragments rel-evant for a given question and using the retrieved information to generate an answer (Chen et al., 2017; Lewis et al., 2020b). However, text-only QA methods often struggle with complex questions requiring multi-hop reasoning on several entities (Yang et al., 2018; Trivedi et al., 2022), symbolic operations such as arithmetic or aggregation (e.g., returning the answer that maximizes a quantity) (Dua et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021), or the composition of multiple intermediate answers (Ho et al., 2020). The challenge of answer-ing these questions is further exacerbated by the 1

fact that retrieving the relevant texts becomes in-creasingly hard as we scale the size of the corpora (Xiong et al., 2021; Weller et al., 2026). To address these problems, recent works have proposed structured indexes, typically graphs, which better capture global connections between documents and entities supporting multi-hop and compositional reasoning (Sarthi et al., 2024; Gutier-rez et al., 2024; Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Edge et al., 2025) and also enable agentic approaches balanc-ing exploration and exploitation on the graph index directly (Gao and Metaxas, 2026; Du et al., 2026). Among these approaches, knowledge graphs (KG) are the most prominent, encoding direct relation-ships between entities as subject-predicate-object triples (Peng et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023). KGs of-fer a formal symbolic relational structure enabling efficient traversal as required by multi-hop ques-tions (Ren et al., 2020; Arakelyan et al., 2021; Galkin et al., 2024; Gregucci et al., 2025). KGs often come with an ontology , which speci-fies a domain-specific schema, including predicates, entity types, and constraints on which type of enti-ties each predicate can link. This ontology enables verifying the consistency of the encoded informa-tion, and can guide the correction of constraint violations (Ahmetaj et al., 2022; Ferranti et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2025). Most importantly, it also makes KGs amenable to efficient retrieval through SQL-like languages such as SPARQL (Harris et al., 2013), which supports arithmetic and aggregation operations useful to answer challenging questions (Gashkov et al., 2025; Perevalov and Both, 2025). Recent works have shown that LLMs can effec-tively extract KGs from text (Zhu et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2025). However, extracting KGs that comply with an existing ontology remains challenging, as it requires balancing expressivity for QA, constraints satisfaction, and tokens usage. One line of work addresses this by including ontology constraints in the LLM context during extraction (Mihinduku-lasooriya et al., 2023; van Cauter and Yakovets, 2024; Nie et al., 2024; Wang and Iwaihara, 2025). However, for large ontologies such as Wikidata (Vrandeˇ ci´ c and Krötzsch, 2014), this introduces a substantial computational overhead due to the required long context size. Moreover, the consis-tency of the extracted KG ultimately depends on the ability of the chosen LLM to enforce the pro-vided constraints, which makes smaller open LLMs perform worse. Although we can leverage ad-hoc models to retrieve relevant ontology fragments for each text, these must be trained for the specific on-tology considered (Zhang and Soh, 2024). Another line of work employs LLMs to extract open-domain triples, which are then iteratively aligned to the on-tology using multiple LLM calls (Arun et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025; Chepurova et al., 2026). While this offers flexibility in encoding relevant information, it requires many LLM calls per extracted triple, leading to a significant cost in terms of tokens. 

Contributions. (i) We propose an efficient method for KG construction from text under ontol-ogy constraints that does not insert the constraints into the LLM context, but rather exploits text em-beddings to map the extracted entity types and pred-icates to the ontology. (ii) We symbolically detect the triples and qualifiers that violate the ontology and selectively correct each of them by prompting an LLM with a candidate set of corrective actions. This allows us to efficiently correct one or many triples (or qualifiers) with a single LLM call. (iii) 

Our experiments show our method to be more to-ken efficient than existing baselines, yet achieving ontology consistency rates for triples and quali-fiers of up to 98.4% and 96.8% respectively. QA experiments further show that our post-extraction corrections preserves crucial information from the text. Then, to assess how amenable the extracted KGs are to symbolic querying via SPARQL, (iv) 

we introduce a benchmark based on graph patterns. Code and data are available at https://github. com/corneliocristina/OAK_MEND .

2 Background: Ontology Constraints 

A KG G is a directed graph where links are en-coded using subject-predicate-object triples: G =

{(si, r i, o i)}i ⊂ E × R × E where E and R de-note the sets of entities and predicates, respectively. KGs may also include qualifiers useful to refine or contextualize the semantics of triples. Given a set of qualifier predicates Q ⊂ R , a qualifier is a pair (rq, o q), with rq ∈ Q , oq ∈ E , that is associated to a single triple. For instance, the sentence “Alan Turing earned his Ph.D. at Prince-ton University in 1938” can be modeled as the triple (Alan Turing , educated at , Princeton Univ. )

equipped with the qualifier (point in time , 1938 ).A key component of a KG is its ontology , which define the admissible entity types and predicates, as well as the constraints they must satisfy. Entity types hierarchy. Let T be a set of en-tity types, and let t1 ≺ t2 denote the “is-subclass-of” partial ordering between types t1, t 2 ∈ T .Moreover, for each entity e ∈ E we denote by 

τ (e) ⊂ T the set of its associated types. For example, mathematician ∈ τ (Alan Turing ) states that Alan Turing is typed as mathematician , while 

mathematician ≺ human specifies that mathemati-cian is a subclass of the type human .

Domain-range constraints. For each predi-cate r ∈ R , let dom (r) ⊆ T and rng (r) ⊆ T 

denote the domain and range constraints of r,respectively. These specify the admissible sub-ject and object types for triples involving r. Atriple (s, r, o ) satisfies the domain constraint if 

ts ≺ us for some ts ∈ τ (s), u s ∈ dom (r), and satisfies the range constraint if to ≺ uo for some 

to ∈ τ (o), u o ∈ rng (r). For example, given 

dom (educated at ) = {human }

(Alan Turing , educated at , Princeton University )

mathematician ≺ human ; and the range constraint whenever university ∈ τ (Princeton University ).While prior work Feng et al. (2024); Chepurova et al. (2026) studied KG extraction under domain-range constraints, here we additionally consider qualifiers constraints, which we formalize next. 

Qualifier constraints. Each predicate r ∈ R 

specifies a set qual (r) ⊆ Q ⊂ R of admissible qualifier predicates. For example, the qualifier 

(point in time , 1938 ) from above is consistent with 

educated at if point in time ∈ qual (educated at ),whereas (color , red ) is inconsistent if color /∈

qual (educated at ). Furthermore, a qualifier (r, o )

satisfies the range constraint of r if to ≺ uo for some to ∈ τ (o), u o ∈ rng (r).

Symbolic querying via SPARQL. The ontology constraints defined above provide the rules neces-sary to write SQL-like queries for symbolic re-trieval or QA, using SPARQL (Harris et al., 2013). 3 KG Extraction and Canonicalization 

Given a source text S, we aim at extracting a KG G

equipped with typed entities and qualifiers such that 3it encodes the relationships between entities in S.Here, we present a method that (1) performs open-domain KG construction, and (2) maps (or canon-icalizes) the extracted open-domain types (resp. predicates) to the types (resp. predicates) allowed by the ontology, i.e., T (resp. R). To make (2) effi-cient, we leverage embeddings computed on types and predicates labels, while we use LLMs for dis-ambiguation only. We dub this method as ontology-aligned KG construction (OAK), and defer the problem of satisfying the ontology constraints to Section 4. In Appendix F we report all our prompts. 

3.1 Open-domain Extraction Step 1. We use a single prompt instructing an LLM to extract triples, entity types, and optionally a num-ber of qualifiers for each triple from the source text 

S. All extracted elements are open-domain: the ontology is not part of the input context, and the extracted types and predicates are instead mapped to it in subsequent steps (see Section 3.2). This KG extraction step differs from the one in Chepurova et al. (2026) by the fact that we also extract the types of qualifier objects, as required by the con-straints over qualifiers (see Section 2). We use a fixed in-context example to improve the adherence to the required JSON output format. Open-domain KG extraction avoids having to first identify which ontology fragments are rele-vant to the input text and include them in the LLM prompt, as done in Zhang and Soh (2024); Feng et al. (2024). As a result, the prompt is shorter, reducing token usage and thereby improving prefix cache hit rates as well. Also, as we show in Sec-tions 5.1 and 5.2, providing ontology constraints in a large input context reduces the number of triples being extracted by reasoning models, which re-duces QA performances. 

3.2 Canonicalization of Types and Predicates Step 2. We map (or canonicalize) the open-domain types and predicates extracted in Step 1 to types and predicates allowed by the ontology, respec-tively. Recently, Chepurova et al. (2026) proposed to perform multiple LLM calls for each extracted triple with the purpose of canonicalizing the subject and object entity types, followed by canonicalizing the predicate such that the resulting triple satis-fies the domain-range constraint. However, this accounts for a significant tokens overhead when applied to several thousands triples. We seek a more token efficient approach by mak-ing the following simplifying assumptions. First, we assume that entities that have been associated to the same open-domain type in Step 1 should have the same canonical type from the ontology. Sec-ond, we assume that extracted triples sharing the same open-domain predicate express the same rela-tionship semantics, and therefore their predicates should be canonicalized in the same way. These as-sumptions avoid performing a high number of LLM calls to canonicalize common types (e.g., the type 

> human

) or predicates multiple times. We exploit these assumptions in a canonicalization step lever-aging embedding similarities, as described next. Formally, let ˆt be a type label that has been ex-tracted in Step 1 , which we aim to map to some 

t ∈ T . We compute the text embedding similar-ity between the extracted ˆt and the label of each 

t ∈ T , and pick the type having the highest simi-larity score. Following Chepurova et al. (2026) we also consider any available label aliases for each t.However, multiple types might have very similar labels or even have common aliases, making canon-icalization through embedding similarities alone insufficient. Whenever multiple types have close similarity scores w.r.t. ˆt based on a hyperparameter 

β, we prompt an LLM to select the most suitable one. We construct the set of candidates types as 

CT (ˆ t) = {ti ∈ T | ϕ(ˆ t, t i) ≥ mˆt − β}

where mˆt = max ti∈T {ϕ(ˆ t, t i)} and ϕ(ˆ t, t i) de-notes the maximum embedding similarity between 

ˆt and any label or alias of ti. Then, the canon-ical t for ˆt is chosen by calling an LLM, i.e., 

t = LLM( S, ˆt, CT (ˆ t), E ˆt), where Eˆt denotes the set of entities that have been previously typed with 

ˆt. In the particular case of |C T (ˆ t)| = 1 , no LLM calls are made and thus β can be used to tune the tokens cost. In our experiments we use the cosine similarity and empirically set β = 0 .05 .We proceed similarly to canonicalize the pred-icates. Given an open-domain predicate label ˆr

extracted in Step 1 , we build the set of candidates 

CR(ˆ r) = {ri ∈ R | ϕ(ˆ r, r i) ≥ mˆr − β},

where mˆr = max ri∈R {ϕ(ˆ r, r i)}. Then, the canon-ical r ∈ R for ˆr is chosen by calling an LLM, i.e., 

r = LLM( S, ˆr, CR(ˆ r), T ˆr), where Tˆr ⊂ E × E de-notes the subject-object entity pairs that have been linked with the open-domain predicate ˆr in Step 1 .Finally, since qualifier predicates specify addi-tional information about the triples themselves, 4their number is typical much smaller than the to-tal number of predicates (e.g., it is only ~29% in the Wikidata ontology fragment we consider in our experiments). This reduces ambiguity during canonicalization, allowing us to simply map each qualifier predicate name to the higher scoring qual-ifier predicate in the ontology. Finally, we remove duplicate entitites as described next. 

3.3 Types-Aware Entity Deduplication Step 3. A common approach to extracting a KG from a corpus consists of splitting the texts into chunks, running the KG extraction method on each chunk and then merging the obtained KGs (Zhang and Soh, 2024; Mo et al., 2025). However, this often yields entities with different labels that refer to the same concept, making an entity deduplica-tion step necessary. Recently, Mo et al. (2025) proposed a two-step algorithm that first clusters en-tities based on their embeddings, and then uses an LLM to iteratively identify duplicates within each cluster. We adopt this method while also exploiting the type information from Step 2 . In particular, entities typed as literals (e.g., a number or a calen-dar date) are excluded from deduplication, hence reducing the clusters size. Until now, we discussed OAK: a method to ex-tract a KG such that entity types and predicates are mapped to the ones in a given ontology. How-ever, the extracted KG might still contain ontology constraint violations, which we aim to correct next. 

4 Correction of Ontology Violations 

The entity types allow us to symbolically detect which triples and qualifiers violate which ontology constraints. This contrasts with methods that rely on LLMs for error detection, such as detecting dis-allowed types (Arun et al., 2025), classifying class membership errors (Allen and Groth, 2024), and supporting human-in-the-loop validation (Regino and dos Reis, 2025). After we collect the triples and qualifiers violat-ing the ontology, we prompt an LLM to correct them by choosing from a set of syntactic trans-formations. Note that, since we correct ontology violations after the KG has been extracted, our method can be applied ex-post to any KG extrac-tion approach, Next, we describe our approach to correct inconsistent triples. 

4.1 Correcting Triples 

To motivate the set of syntactic transformations useful to correct ontology violations, we identify a number of KG extraction mistakes – each of which suggesting a particular corrective action. 

V1. The subject-object inversion error oc-curs when the types of the subject s (resp. object o) in a triple (s, r, o ) are inconsistent with the domain (resp. range) of the predi-cate r because the triple direction is reversed, while the inverted triple (o, r, s ) is instead consistent. For example, if dom (author ) =

{book } and rng (author ) = {human }, then the triple (G. Orwell , author , Animal Farm ) is incon-sistent when τ (G. Orwell ) = {human } and 

τ (Animal Farm ) = {book }. Although this triple expresses that G. Orwell is the author of Animal Farm , it violates the ontology because the subject and object are reversed. It can therefore be cor-rected by swapping subject and object . We find this mistake to be frequent, and thus automatically apply the correction whenever possible. 

V2. The error of predicate mis-canonicalization 

occurs when a predicate has been wrongly selected: the triple (s, r, o ) is inconsistent because r does not explain a relationship be-tween entities s and o. For example, the triple 

(Animal Farm , distribution format , Penguin Books )

might have been hallucinated because the publisher  

> Penguin Books

has “Books” in its label. This triple is inconsistent because the type organi-zation of Penguin Books is not allowed by the range constraint of distribution format . Instead, 

(Animal Farm , publisher , Penguin Books ) satisfies the domain-range constraint of publisher . Thus, the corrective action is to replace the predicate .

V3. Lastly, a triple (s, r, o ) may be inconsis-tent because of underspecified or missing types :the types of the subject s (resp. the object 

o) are not specific enough to satisfy the do-main (resp. the range) of r or, alternatively, a type might be missing. For example, as-suming that τ (Animal Farm ) = {written work },then (Animal Farm , author , G. Orwell ) is inconsis-tent with the domain of author , as book ≺

5.3 Q3. Patterns Enabling SPARQL Queries 

We evaluate how amenable the extracted KGs are to SPARQL queries, for which consistency with the underlying ontology is crucial (see Section 2). To disentangle our evaluation with the challenging task of translating questions in natural language to SPARQL (Gashkov et al., 2025; Perevalov and Both, 2025), we introduce a benchmark measur-ing the occurrence of basic graph patterns (BGPs). BGPs are abstract sub-graphs defined as a con-junction of triples forming the WHERE clause in a SPARQL query (e.g., the query in Section 2). Hence, their presence in the extracted KGs is a nec-essary condition for executing SPARQL queries. 

BGPs datasets. We build a dataset of BGPs of queries executed on the Wikidata endpoint, which we extract from the LSQ-2.0 collection (Saleem et al., 2015; Stadler et al., 2024). This yields a set of a few thousands BGPs over hundreds of predicates. Moreover, to obtain a larger set of patterns, we arti-ficially generate BGPs from the ontology domain-range constraints using templates common in the complex query answering literature (Gregucci et al., 2025). We detail the procedures and the statistics of the two BGPs datasets in Appendix B. 

Metrics. One way to evaluate the extracted KGs would be to simply compare the total frequency of BGP matches. However, since some BGPs appear much more frequently than others, we use metrics that also account for rare BGPs. Inspired by the h-index and i10-index metrics, which reward a con-sistent output of highly cited papers, we adopt anal-ogous metrics to reward methods that match many BGPs with consistently high frequency across a di-verse set of patterns. In Table 3 we report h-index and i100-index metrics computed on all patterns, to-gether with the average number of links connecting two entities (edge multiplicity), and the the percent-age of connected subject-object pairs with two or more links. The last two metrics serve as a sanity check for redundant triples that encode the same information with different predicates, since such redundancy could artificially boost BGPs matches at the cost of introducing ambiguity. 

Results. Our OAK+MEND on Q WEN 3-30B-A3B is second only to the I N-C ONTEXT baseline, which however has high edge multiplicity. As we detail in Table E.5, this is because I N-C ONTEXT 

extracts highly-redundant triples over pairs of enti-ties encoding the same information, yet with differ-ent predicates that have slightly different meaning. Instead, on GPT-OSS-120B, our OAK+MEND achieves similar performances to W IKONTIC , and much better than I N-C ONTEXT , without having an outlier edge multiplicity. In all cases our ontology-based corrections boost h-index. In Appendix E and Appendix E we report the results achieved on only the LSQ-2.0-based or artificial patterns, re-spectively. 

6 Conclusion 

We introduced a method for ontology-grounded KG extraction that corrects ontology violations 

ex-post , improving token efficiency compared to existing methods based on repeated LLM calls or long ontology-aware contexts. Our approach is sup-8ported by experiments evaluating the many facets of KG extraction: ontology consistency, QA perfor-mances, token efficiency, and suitability for sym-bolic querying. In particular, we have shown that targeted LLM-predicted corrections substantially improve ontology consistency while preserving se-mantic accuracy. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to study KG extraction through such a broad empirical evaluation, while also pro-viding an in-depth evaluation over the Wikidata ontology. Finally, we believe our SPARQL graph-patterns benchmark can serve as a useful tool for quantitatively assessing how well a KG extraction method supports SPARQL-based querying. 

Limitations 

Our experiments focused on the general-purpose ontology available in Wikidata, as this is a popular setting in the KG extraction community. Instead, one can consider custom-built ontologies that may be specific for a certain domain, e.g., for biomed-ical KGs (Walsh et al., 2020). We believe under-standing whether the performances achieved by LLMs generalize to custom or even closed-source ontologies remains poorly understood. In addition, our method requires a detailed ontology specifica-tion, including both domain-range and qualifiers constraints, which might not be available. Moreover, in our experiments we restricted our-selves to open-weight LLMs only. While this pro-motes reproducibility and transparency over time, further experiments exploiting closed-weight yet more powerful LLMs could provide a more de-tailed overview of the performances achieved by the different KG extraction methods. Lastly, our work lacks a comparison measuring QA performances achieved by means of SPARQL queries executed on the extracted KGs. This is because it would require experimenting with text-to-SPARQL translation techniques to convert questions in natural language to SPARQL queries (Gashkov et al., 2025; Perevalov and Both, 2025). Since this is already a very challenging open prob-lem, in our work we instead rely on proxy metrics based on the occurrences of SPARQL query graph patterns alone.

The idea of our benchmark based on BGPs is to measure how many times this graph pattern matches with an extracted KG, i.e., counting the number of distinct assignments to the person, occupation and award entity variables. The higher is the number of matching BGPs, the higher is the likelihood that a SPARQL query having such BGP will return a non-empty result. In other words, the existence of BGPs in a KG is a necessary—although not sufficient—condition for retrieving relevant information by means of a SPARQL query. This setting is similar to another one used to evaluate graph generative models, e.g., where the percentage of graphs having certain graph motifs as in molecules generation is computed (Li et al., 2018). Similarly, other works compare the divergence between the distribution of the number of graph motifs in the ground truth and in the generated graphs (Hocevar and Demsar, 2014; You et al., 2018). However, the difference is that in our case we do not have access to the ground truth KG. Therefore, inspired by h-index and i k-index citation metrics rewarding an author for having many citations over a diverse set of papers, we use the same metrics to reward a KG extraction method for having a high number of matches over a diverse set of BGPs. In our case, a method achieves an h-index of n if there are at least n BGPs appearing with a frequency of n or above in the extracted KGs. Moreover, a method achieves an i k-index of n if there are at least n BGPs with a frequency equal or greater than k. To compute the number of matching BGPs, we filter out the extracted triples violating the ontology, and recover the frequency of each BGP using a SPARQL run on a qlever SPARQL endpoint. Next, we detail two datasets of BGPs we use: one extracted from real-world queries run on the Wikidata SPARQL endpoint, and one artificially constructed from the considered domain-range ontology constraints. Table B.1 shows statistics about both BGPs datasets. 

Real-world BGPs. We extract BGPs from real-world SPARQL queries on Wikidata, which are 13 collected under the Linked Dataset SPARQL Query Logs (LSQ-2.0) (Saleem et al., 2015; Stadler et al., 2024). We do so by running SPARQL queries on the LSQ-2.0 publicly available endpoint. After we collect a BGP, we replace constant entities in it with fresh variables and filter it out only if it contains multiple triples with the same predicate. This allows us to only keep interesting BGPs that do not map to a specific SPARQL query with bound entities. We then remove duplicate BGPs via a directed graph isometry test. The obtained dataset consists of 5757 BGPs over 716 Wikidata predicates. 

Artificial BGPs. To build a larger dataset of BGPs, we artificially construct them by exploiting common Wikidata predicates and BGP structures commonly used in existing complex query answering benchmarks (Gregucci et al., 2025). We start by building a special KG—called ontology KG —encoding the domain-range constraints over a set of popular Wikidata predicates, which we take from the DocRED (Yao et al., 2019) and CodRED (Yao et al., 2021) datasets. That is, we construct a KG such that for each predicate r specifying at least one between the domain and range constraints, we insert a triple (ts, r, t o)

where ts ∈ dom (r) (resp. to ∈ rng (r)). If the domain (resp. range) is not specified, we introduce a fresh “any” type node in the ontology KG. Then, we run SPARQL queries on such KG in order to exhaustively recover BGPs with ground predicates having various structures corresponding to complex queries. Fig. A.1 shows the different BGP structures we consider useful to construct SPARQL queries. For computational reasons we randomly sample up to 10 ′000 BGPs per structure. This yields a set of BGPs having various kinds of structures and with different combinations of predicates that are consistent withe domain-range ontology constraints. We summarize the BGPs structures and their count in Table B.2. 

C Detailed Description of the Qualifiers Correction Step 

After we corrected the triples violating domain-range constraints in Section 4.1, we can now correct any attached qualifier violating the ontology. Similarly to our description of triples correction in Section 4.1, here we describe a collection of possible KG extraction mistakes that make qualifiers violating the ontology. Each of these possible mistakes will then motivate the corrective actions that an LLM will be prompted to choose from. 

V4. A qualifier (rq, o q) that is attached to a triple (s, r, o ) is inconsistent because rq is not a quali-fier predicate allowed by the triple predicate r, i.e., rq̸ ∈ qual (r). For example, in Wikidata we have that location /∈ qual (publication date ), i.e., location is not a qualifier predicate allowed by pub-lication date . Therefore, the qualifier (location , London ) cannot be a consistent when attached to the triple (Animal Farm , publication date , 1945 ). Instead, place of publication ∈ qual (publication date ) is an allowed qualifier and therefore it should be used instead of location as qualifier predicate. The corrective action here is replacing the qualifier predicate with one allowed by the predicate in the triple .

V5. A qualifier (rq, o q) of a triple (s, r, o ) might not be consistent with respect to the range constraint of rq,and the reason is that rq is a qualifier predicate that has been mis-canonicalized . That is, we have that 

rq ∈ qual (r), yet the types of the entity oq are incosnsistent w.r.t. the range of r. For example, consider the qualifier (distribution format , Penguin Books ) attached (Animal Farm , publication date , 1945 ). This qual-ifier is inconsistent if τ (Penguin Books ) = {publisher }, since it would not satisfy the range constraint of 

> distribution format

, i.e., expecting a format. Yet, we have that distribution format ∈ qual (publication date ).The correcting action is replacing the qualifier predicate with publisher , which is allowed by the predicate  

> publication date

and it is consistent with the type of Penguin Books .

V6. Finally, similarly to the underspecified or missing entity types mistake discussed for triples in Section 4.1, a qualifier (rq, o q) might not be consistent because of the same reason, i.e., the types of oq

are not specific enough, or a type is missing, in order to be consistent with the range of rq.As detailed in Section 4.2, these possible mistakes suggest us to prompt an LLM to select between two syntactic transformations of the qualifier, namely replace_predicate and add_object_type .

D LLM-based Baseline with In-Context Ontology Constraints 

We detail our KG extraction baseline inserting the ontology constraints as part of the input context, which we denote simply as I N-C ONTEXT , and report the prompts in Appendix F.3. 14 The idea of I N-C ONTEXT is to (1) firstly predict a relevant fragment of the ontology consisting of predicates and allowed qualifiers given the source text; and (2) extract the KG from the source text by instructing the LLM to comply with the ontology. For (1), we prompt an LLM to extract open-domain predicates given the source text. Then, the extracted open-domain predicates are mapped to the top-k most similar predicates allowed by ontology, in terms of embedding similarity computed over predicate labels and aliases. We use k = 20 in our experiments. Then, for (2) we insert a verbalization of the predicted predicates, relative domain-range constraints, and allowed qualifiers as part of the LLM context upon KG extraction. If an extracted predicate (resp. entity type) is not one of the allowed ones (resp. one appearing in a domain or range of a predicate), we still keep it as an unknown predicate (resp. entity type) labeled with the same name.
